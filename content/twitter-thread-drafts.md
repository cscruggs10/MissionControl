### Tweet 2 (What It Is)
The Augusta Rule (IRC Section 280A) lets you rent your home for up to 14 days per year without reporting the rental income.

Named after Augusta, GA where homeowners rent during the Masters golf tournament.

The IRS simply doesn't tax that income. Period.

Here's how it works:

Your business pays YOU fair market rent for legitimate business use of your home (board meetings, planning sessions, client entertainment).

You receive the payment.

You keep it tax-free.

Win-win.

## Thread #2: Entity Structure Mistake ($52K Cost)

### Tweet 1 (Hook)
Choosing S-Corp election at the wrong time cost my client $52,379 in one year.

Here's the entity timing trap that bleeds six figures — and why conventional CPA wisdom gets this wrong 🧵

**Character count:** 213

---

### Tweet 2 (Meet the Client)
Real case study (details changed for privacy):

Client: $180K revenue in Year 1, growing fast
Industry: SaaS consulting  
Tax advisor: "You need to elect S-Corp NOW to save on self-employment tax"

Reasonable advice. But wrong timing.

**Character count:** 229

---

### Tweet 3 (The Decision)
The CPA's reasoning was sound:

S-Corp lets you split income into salary + distributions.
Only the salary portion pays self-employment tax (15.3%).
More distributions = less SE tax.

Classic tax-saving move... right?

Not when you're under the QBI threshold.

**Character count:** 256

---

### Tweet 4 (What They Didn't Know)
What the CPA missed: Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction.

If you're under $383K taxable income (2026 threshold), you get a 20% deduction on qualified business income.

S-Corp distributions DON'T qualify for QBI.
Sole prop/LLC income DOES.

That's a huge difference.

**Character count:** 273

---

### Tweet 5 (The Math)
Here's the actual cost:

**As Sole Prop:**
$180K income × 20% QBI = $36K deduction
Tax savings: ~$13,320

**As S-Corp:**
$80K salary + $100K distributions
QBI limited to $0
Lost deduction cost: ~$13,320

Plus S-Corp compliance: $3K-$5K/year.

Total Year 1 hit: $16K-$18K.

**Character count:** 262

---

### Tweet 6 (When S-Corp DOES Make Sense)
S-Corp becomes optimal when:

✅ Income exceeds QBI phase-out ($383K+)
✅ Self-employment tax savings outweigh QBI loss
✅ Reasonable comp ratio justified
✅ Administrative costs worth it

The break-even point varies by business — but it's NOT automatic at $50K revenue.

**Character count:** 271

---

### Tweet 7 (Break-Even Framework)
Simple decision framework:

**Under $250K income:** Probably stay LLC/sole prop (QBI valuable)
**$250K-$383K:** Run the math (gray zone)
**Above $383K:** S-Corp likely wins (QBI phases out)

But exceptions exist. Entity choice is multi-variable, not one-size-fits-all.

### Tweet 8 (The Fix)
How we fixed it:

1. Reverted S-Corp election (IRS allows within 2.5 months)
2. Optimized as LLC with QBI maximization
3. Added captive insurance layer ($800K deduction)
4. Planned S-Corp timing for Year 3 (above QBI threshold)

3-year savings: $127K.

Entity structure isn't a "set it and forget it" decision.

It's a timing strategy.

Reactive tax compliance asks "What entity?"
Proactive tax strategy asks "What entity, WHEN?"

Wrong timing = leaving six figures on the table.
